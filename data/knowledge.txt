## 1. Icons vs Idolatry

Orthodox Christians distinguish between worship, which belongs to God alone, and veneration, which is honor given to holy people and sacred objects that point toward God. Icons are not considered idols or false gods, but visual testimonies of the Incarnation of Christ and the holiness of those transformed by Him. Since God truly became man in Jesus Christ, the invisible God entered visible history, making depiction possible. The Orthodox Church also points to Old Testament examples where God commanded sacred images such as the cherubim on the Ark of the Covenant and the decorations in the Temple, proving that not every religious image is idolatry. An idol is worship of creation instead of the Creator, while an icon directs the believer toward God, spiritual contemplation, prayer, and remembrance of divine truth. The Seventh Ecumenical Council defended the use of icons by teaching that honor given to the image passes to the prototype, meaning the respect shown to an icon ultimately refers to Christ, the saints, or the holy events represented.

---

## 2. Scripture vs Holy Tradition

Orthodoxy teaches that Holy Scripture and Holy Tradition are not opposing authorities but parts of the same divine revelation preserved within the life of the Church. The Bible itself emerged from the Church, as the apostles preached the Gospel orally before the New Testament books were written and later canonized. Tradition includes the teachings of the apostles, the writings of the Church Fathers, the Ecumenical Councils, liturgical worship, and the continuous life of the Church guided by the Holy Spirit. Orthodox Christians argue that the doctrine of “Bible alone” is not explicitly taught in Scripture and historically led to countless interpretations and divisions. Instead, the Church serves as the guardian and interpreter of Scripture, preserving the faith passed down from the apostles without corruption. Tradition is therefore not seen as human invention but as the living continuity of apostolic Christianity through history.

---

## 3. Intercession of Saints

The Orthodox Church teaches that the saints are alive in Christ and continue to participate in the spiritual life of the Church even after physical death. Asking saints for intercession is understood similarly to asking fellow Christians on earth to pray for one another, except the saints are believed to be perfected in holiness and closer to God. Orthodoxy rejects the idea that death separates believers from the Body of Christ, since Christ conquered death through His resurrection. Biblical references such as the prayers of the righteous, the heavenly witnesses surrounding believers, and scenes in Revelation showing heavenly prayers being offered before God are used to support this understanding. The saints are not worshiped or viewed as independent sources of power, but as faithful servants of God whose prayers are effective because of their closeness to Him. This practice has existed since the early centuries of Christianity and remains deeply connected to Orthodox spirituality and communal understanding of salvation.

---

## 4. Theotokos (Mary as Mother of God)

The title “Theotokos,” meaning “God-bearer” or “Mother of God,” was affirmed by the Church to protect the truth about Jesus Christ rather than merely to exalt Mary. Orthodoxy teaches that Jesus Christ is fully God and fully man united in one person, and therefore the one whom Mary gave birth to was truly God incarnate. Calling Mary “Mother of God” does not mean she existed before God or created the divine nature, but that she gave birth to the incarnate Son of God according to His humanity. The Church honors Mary because of her unique role in salvation history, her obedience to God, and her example of holiness and humility. Orthodox Christians also see Mary as the fulfillment of Old Testament symbols such as the Ark of the Covenant and the living temple of God. Her importance ultimately points believers toward Christ and the mystery of the Incarnation.

---

## 5. Prayer for the Dead

Orthodox Christianity practices prayer for the departed because the Church believes that love and communion in Christ continue beyond death. Since salvation is understood as an ongoing participation in God’s mercy and life, the faithful pray that God grants rest, forgiveness, and peace to departed souls. Biblical examples, early Christian writings, and ancient liturgical practices are often cited to support this tradition. Orthodoxy rejects the idea that death completely isolates believers from the prayers of the Church, because all Christians remain united in Christ as one Body. Prayer for the dead is not seen as an attempt to override God’s judgment but as an expression of hope, mercy, and love rooted in God’s compassion. This practice also reflects the Orthodox understanding that human beings continue to spiritually encounter God after death while awaiting the final resurrection and judgment.

---

## 6. Infant Baptism

Orthodox Christians baptize infants because baptism is understood as entry into the covenant community and participation in the life of Christ rather than merely a public declaration of personal belief. Just as children in the Old Testament entered the covenant through circumcision, infants in the Church are welcomed into the new covenant through baptism. Orthodoxy teaches that baptism grants spiritual rebirth, cleansing from sin, union with Christ, and incorporation into the Church. Since salvation is viewed as both personal and communal, children are not excluded from receiving divine grace simply because they cannot yet intellectually articulate faith. The faith of the Church, represented by parents and godparents, supports the child until personal spiritual maturity develops. Historically, infant baptism has existed since the earliest centuries of Christianity and reflects the belief that God’s grace is freely given and not limited by age.

---

## 7. Real Presence in the Eucharist

Orthodoxy teaches that the Eucharist is truly the Body and Blood of Jesus Christ and not merely a symbolic memorial meal. During the Divine Liturgy, through the action of the Holy Spirit, the bread and wine become genuine participation in Christ Himself while still retaining their outward physical appearance. This belief is rooted in Christ’s words at the Last Supper and reinforced by the teachings of the apostles and early Church Fathers. The Eucharist is viewed as a profound mystery through which believers receive spiritual nourishment, forgiveness, unity with Christ, and participation in eternal life. Orthodox Christians reject purely symbolic interpretations because they believe the early Church consistently treated Communion with deep reverence as something sacred and truly divine. Participation in the Eucharist is therefore central to Orthodox worship and spiritual life.

---

## 8. Faith vs Faith and Works

Orthodoxy teaches that salvation comes through God’s grace and not by human effort alone, yet genuine faith naturally produces works of love, obedience, repentance, and transformation. The Orthodox Church rejects both legalistic self-salvation and the idea that intellectual belief alone guarantees salvation regardless of one’s life. Instead, salvation is understood as synergy, meaning cooperation between divine grace and human freedom. Faith is living and active, expressing itself through prayer, humility, charity, fasting, worship, and perseverance in Christ. Works are not viewed as earning salvation but as evidence of a transformed life united with God. The teachings of Christ, the apostles, and the early Church consistently present salvation as a lifelong process of spiritual growth and communion with God rather than a one-time legal declaration.

---

## 9. Theosis (Deification)

Theosis is the Orthodox teaching that human beings are called to participate in the divine life of God through union with Christ. This does not mean humans become gods by nature or equal to God, but that they are transformed by God’s grace and share in His holiness, love, immortality, and spiritual life. Theosis is rooted in the Incarnation, because Christ became man so humanity could be restored and united with God. Through prayer, sacraments, repentance, ascetic struggle, and the work of the Holy Spirit, believers gradually grow into the likeness of Christ. Orthodoxy views salvation not merely as forgiveness of sins but as healing, transformation, and participation in divine communion. This teaching has deep roots in Scripture and the writings of the Church Fathers and remains one of the central themes of Orthodox spirituality.

---

## 10. Apostolic Succession

Orthodoxy teaches that the authority and teachings of the Church have been preserved continuously from the apostles through apostolic succession, especially through bishops ordained in an unbroken historical line. This continuity is not merely organizational but spiritual and doctrinal, ensuring that the faith taught today remains connected to the original apostolic Church established by Christ. Bishops are seen as successors to the apostles who safeguard doctrine, administer sacraments, and maintain unity within the Church. Orthodox Christians argue that Christianity was never intended to exist as isolated individuals independently interpreting Scripture, but as a visible, sacramental community guided by ordained leadership. Apostolic succession therefore serves as protection against false teachings and doctrinal innovation while preserving the integrity of the Church across generations.
## 11. Sola Scriptura

Orthodoxy rejects the doctrine of Sola Scriptura, or “Scripture alone,” because the Bible itself was produced, preserved, and canonized within the life of the Church. The apostles preached the Gospel orally before the New Testament was written, and many teachings of the Christian faith were transmitted through worship, liturgy, and apostolic instruction long before a formal biblical canon existed. Orthodox Christians argue that Scripture never teaches that it is the sole authority for Christian doctrine, but instead points believers toward the Church as the “pillar and ground of the truth.” The Church Fathers, Ecumenical Councils, and apostolic tradition are therefore viewed as essential for properly interpreting Scripture. Orthodoxy also points to the fragmentation within Protestantism as evidence that individual interpretation without authoritative tradition can lead to doctrinal confusion and division. Scripture is fully inspired and authoritative, but it is understood within the living tradition of the Church rather than isolated from it.

---

## 12. Once Saved Always Saved

Orthodoxy rejects the idea that salvation, once received, can never be lost regardless of how a person later lives or believes. Instead, salvation is understood as an ongoing relationship and journey of communion with God that requires perseverance, repentance, faithfulness, and spiritual growth. While God is always faithful and desires the salvation of humanity, human beings still possess free will and can choose to reject or abandon their relationship with Him. The Orthodox Church points to biblical passages warning believers against falling away, becoming spiritually hardened, or abandoning the faith as evidence that salvation is not treated as an irreversible legal status. Assurance in Orthodoxy is found not in presumption but in continual trust in God’s mercy and active participation in the spiritual life of the Church. Salvation is therefore seen as dynamic and relational rather than a one-time guarantee detached from future choices.

---

## 13. Confession to a Priest

Orthodox Christians confess sins to a priest because the priest serves as a witness, spiritual guide, and representative of the Church while God remains the true forgiver of sins. Christ granted His apostles authority to bind and loose sins, and Orthodoxy believes this ministry continues within the sacramental life of the Church through apostolic succession. Confession is not viewed as replacing direct prayer to God, since believers are encouraged to repent personally every day, but as a sacramental act of humility, accountability, healing, and reconciliation with both God and the Church community. The priest offers guidance, prayers, encouragement, and absolution while helping believers confront spiritual struggles honestly. Orthodoxy sees sin not merely as breaking rules but as a spiritual illness that requires healing, and confession is therefore understood as part of the therapeutic and restorative process of salvation. This practice has existed since the early Church and reflects the communal nature of Christian life.

---

## 14. Church Authority

Orthodoxy teaches that Christ established a visible and unified Church with spiritual authority rather than leaving Christianity to individual interpretation alone. The apostles were given responsibility to teach, preserve doctrine, administer sacraments, and guide believers, and this authority continued through bishops and the life of the Church. Orthodox Christians believe that the Holy Spirit protects the Church from falling into complete doctrinal error, especially through the Ecumenical Councils and apostolic tradition. Church authority is not viewed as political domination but as spiritual guardianship and preservation of truth. Without a visible authority, Orthodoxy argues that endless competing interpretations of Christianity emerge, leading to fragmentation and confusion. The Church therefore serves as the historical and spiritual continuation of the apostolic community founded by Christ, preserving unity in faith, worship, and doctrine across generations.

---

## 15. Cross vs Resurrection Emphasis

Orthodoxy places strong emphasis on both the Cross and the Resurrection, but especially highlights the Resurrection as Christ’s victory over death, sin, and corruption. While the crucifixion reveals Christ’s sacrificial love and obedience, the Resurrection demonstrates the defeat of death itself and the restoration of humanity. Orthodox theology tends to focus less on purely legal explanations of salvation and more on healing, transformation, and liberation from death and spiritual bondage. Through His death and resurrection, Christ enters into the depths of human suffering and overcomes it from within, opening the path to eternal life for humanity. This is why Orthodox worship, especially during Pascha, celebrates joyfully the triumph of life over death. The Cross and Resurrection are inseparable mysteries, together forming the foundation of salvation and the hope of humanity’s restoration.

---

## 16. Papal Supremacy

Orthodoxy rejects the doctrine that the Bishop of Rome possesses universal jurisdiction and supreme authority over the entire Church. While the Orthodox Church historically recognizes Rome as holding a place of honor among the ancient patriarchates, it does not accept later developments that granted the pope absolute authority or infallibility over all Christians. Orthodox Christians argue that authority in the early Church functioned through councils, conciliar agreement, and the collective witness of bishops rather than centralized papal control. The Ecumenical Councils are seen as the highest authority in preserving doctrine, guided by the Holy Spirit and the consensus of the Church. Orthodoxy maintains that no single bishop can stand above the Church as its sole earthly head, since Christ Himself is the head of the Church. The disagreement over papal supremacy became one of the major causes of the Great Schism between East and West.

---

## 17. Filioque Controversy

The Filioque controversy concerns the phrase “and the Son,” which was later added in the West to the Nicene Creed regarding the procession of the Holy Spirit. The original creed stated that the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father, and Orthodoxy maintains that this wording should remain unchanged because it reflects the teaching of Scripture and the theology of the early Ecumenical Councils. Orthodox Christians argue that altering the creed without an Ecumenical Council was improper and that the Filioque risks confusing the distinct relationships within the Holy Trinity. The Father is understood as the sole source within the Trinity, while the Son is eternally begotten of the Father and the Spirit eternally proceeds from the Father. Although Orthodoxy acknowledges the close relationship between the Son and the Spirit, it rejects formulations that appear to compromise the monarchy of the Father within Trinitarian theology. This issue became one of the major theological disagreements between Eastern Orthodoxy and Roman Catholicism.

---

## 18. Original Sin vs Ancestral Sin

Orthodoxy teaches ancestral sin rather than the Western concept of inherited guilt associated with original sin. According to Orthodox theology, humanity inherited the consequences of Adam and Eve’s fall, such as mortality, corruption, suffering, and inclination toward sin, but not personal guilt for Adam’s transgression. Each individual is responsible for their own sins rather than being born already condemned for another person’s actions. The fall damaged human nature and separated humanity from the fullness of communion with God, making people vulnerable to sin and death. Christ came not only to forgive sins but to heal human nature, conquer death, and restore humanity’s relationship with God. Salvation in Orthodoxy is therefore viewed more as healing and restoration than as satisfaction of inherited legal guilt. This distinction shapes many other aspects of Orthodox theology concerning baptism, salvation, and human nature.

---

## 19. The Wider Ethiopian Canon

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church possesses one of the largest biblical canons in Christianity, including books not found in most Protestant or Catholic Bibles. Orthodox Ethiopians believe these books were preserved through the historical tradition and spiritual life of the Ethiopian Church and continue to hold theological and liturgical importance. The broader canon includes texts such as Enoch, Jubilees, and additional writings valued in Ethiopian Christianity for centuries. The Ethiopian Church argues that the biblical canon developed gradually within the life of the Church and that different ancient Christian traditions historically preserved slightly different collections of sacred books. These texts are seen not as later inventions but as part of a longstanding heritage received and maintained through ecclesiastical tradition. The wider canon reflects the unique historical development and theological identity of Ethiopian Orthodoxy.

---

## 20. Sabbath and Sunday Observance

The Ethiopian Orthodox tradition uniquely honors both the Sabbath on Saturday and the Lord’s Day on Sunday, though Sunday remains the primary celebration of Christ’s resurrection. Saturday is respected in remembrance of God’s rest after creation and because it has longstanding roots in biblical and early Christian practice, while Sunday commemorates the resurrection of Christ and the beginning of the new creation. Ethiopian Orthodoxy does not generally treat Saturday observance as a rejection of Christianity or a return to Old Testament legalism, but as part of its ancient spiritual heritage and liturgical rhythm. The Church sees continuity rather than contradiction between honoring the Sabbath and celebrating the resurrection on Sunday. This dual observance reflects the distinctive historical development of Ethiopian Christianity and its emphasis on preserving ancient traditions passed down through generations.
